Writing sample

VCE Biology — Blood glucose regulation as negative feedback

VCAA Study Design 2022–2026 — Unit 1, Area of Study 2

Curriculum positioning

VCE Biology — Unit 1, Area of Study 2 (current 2022–2026 Study Design)
Broad focus: How do plant and animal systems function?
Key knowledge addressed: regulation of body temperature, blood glucose and water balance in animals by homeostatic mechanisms, including stimulus-response models, feedback loops and associated organ structures; malfunctions in homeostatic mechanisms (e.g. type 1 diabetes, hypoglycaemia).
Worked example: regulation of blood glucose by insulin and glucagon

This sample is designed to show how a physiological process can be taught as a cause-and-effect system rather than as a list of hormones to memorise. Blood glucose regulation is familiar enough for students to picture, but rich enough to teach stimulus, receptor/control centre, signal, effector and response.

Learning objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to:

  1. describe homeostasis as the maintenance of internal conditions within a suitable range;
  2. identify the stimulus, receptor/control centre, hormone signal, target tissue and response in blood glucose regulation;
  3. compare the roles of insulin and glucagon;
  4. explain why blood glucose regulation is an example of negative feedback; and
  5. use graph evidence to support a biological explanation.

Prior knowledge

Students should already understand that:

  • cells need glucose for cellular respiration;
  • hormones are chemical signals carried in the blood;
  • organs and tissues can respond to signals in specific ways;
  • the liver and muscles can store glucose as glycogen.

1. Text lesson — Your body is not trying to draw a flat line

Why this matters

After lunch, your blood glucose rises. During exercise, it may fall. That is normal. A healthy body is not trying to keep blood glucose perfectly flat every minute of the day.

Homeostasis is more realistic than that. It keeps internal conditions within a safe working range, even as life keeps changing around us: meals, sleep, stress, movement and growth.

This is a good example of the quiet work your body does all the time. You do not have to think, "I should release insulin now." The system senses the change and responds.

The big idea

Blood glucose regulation is a negative feedback system.

Negative feedback means the response reduces the original change:

  • if blood glucose is too high, the response lowers it;
  • if blood glucose is too low, the response raises it.

The goal is not a perfectly fixed number. The goal is to bring blood glucose back toward the normal range.

The whole system fits into the stimulus-response model used throughout VCE Biology:

stimulus → receptor → signal (hormone) → effector → response → return toward normal range.

When blood glucose is too high

Step 1 — Stimulus

After a meal, carbohydrates are digested into glucose. Glucose is absorbed into the blood, so blood glucose concentration rises.

The stimulus is high blood glucose.

Step 2 — Detection and signal

Cells in the pancreas detect the rise. Beta cells release the hormone insulin into the blood.

Insulin is the signal that says, in effect: there is extra glucose available; store or use it.

Step 3 — Target tissues respond

Insulin acts on target tissues such as the liver, skeletal muscle and adipose tissue. These tissues take up more glucose from the blood.

In the liver and muscles, glucose can be joined together and stored as glycogen. This process is called glycogenesis.

Step 4 — Negative feedback

As glucose moves out of the blood and into cells, blood glucose concentration falls back toward the normal range. The response has reduced the original change.

That is negative feedback.

Quick check 1

A student writes, "Insulin breaks down glycogen to raise blood glucose." What is wrong with this statement?

Answer: Insulin lowers blood glucose. It promotes glucose uptake and glycogen formation. Glucagon is the hormone that helps raise blood glucose when it is too low.

When blood glucose is too low

Step 1 — Stimulus

Between meals or during exercise, cells continue using glucose. Blood glucose concentration can fall below the normal range.

The stimulus is low blood glucose.

Step 2 — Detection and signal

Alpha cells in the pancreas detect the fall and release the hormone glucagon.

Glucagon is the signal that says, in effect: blood glucose is running low; release stored glucose.

Step 3 — Target tissues respond

Glucagon mainly acts on the liver. It stimulates the breakdown of glycogen into glucose. This process is called glycogenolysis. The liver can then release glucose into the blood.

Glucagon can also support gluconeogenesis, the production of glucose from non-carbohydrate molecules.

Step 4 — Negative feedback

As the liver releases glucose, blood glucose concentration rises back toward the normal range. The response has reduced the original change.

Quick check 2

Complete the pathway:

Low blood glucose → pancreas releases ________ → liver breaks down ________ → blood glucose ________.

Answer: glucagon; glycogen; rises/increases.

Common misconception

Misconception: Homeostasis means the body keeps conditions exactly constant.

Correction: Homeostasis keeps conditions within a suitable range. Blood glucose rises and falls during the day. Negative feedback prevents the level from moving too far away from the range needed for normal cell function.

Worked example — reading a graph (equivalent to a ~4-mark data-interpretation response)

A student's blood glucose is measured before and after lunch. The graph shows blood glucose rising for about 45 minutes after eating, then gradually returning toward the starting range over the next two hours. Insulin concentration rises shortly after blood glucose begins to rise.

Question: Explain the pattern using insulin and negative feedback.

Worked response:
After lunch, carbohydrates are digested and glucose is absorbed into the blood, causing blood glucose to rise. Beta cells in the pancreas detect the increase and release insulin — which is why insulin concentration rises shortly after the glucose peak. Insulin travels in the blood to target tissues such as liver and muscle. These cells take up more glucose, and some glucose is stored as glycogen. As glucose is removed from the blood, blood glucose falls back toward the normal range over the following two hours. This is negative feedback because the response (insulin action) reduces the original change (rising blood glucose).

Why this is a strong answer:
It uses the graph pattern — the timing of the insulin rise relative to glucose, and the gradual fall over two hours — rather than ignoring the data. It links each part of the curve to a biological step.

Human context

Blood glucose regulation is not just a diagram in a textbook. It affects how people feel, concentrate, exercise and stay well. When this system does not work properly, as in type 1 diabetes, daily life can involve monitoring glucose, planning food and activity, and using medication or insulin.

A careful biology explanation should therefore be accurate without being judgemental. The aim is to understand the regulation system, not to blame people for a health condition.

2. Video script — Blood glucose regulation in about 115 seconds

Time Narration On-screen / visual direction
0–12 s "Your blood glucose is not meant to be a perfectly flat line. It rises after meals and falls between them. Homeostasis keeps it within a safe working range." Graph with a shaded normal range; line rises and falls within/near it.
12–27 s "After a meal, glucose enters the blood. The stimulus is high blood glucose." Food → intestine → glucose entering bloodstream. Label: stimulus.
27–43 s "Beta cells in the pancreas detect the rise and release insulin. Insulin is a hormone signal carried in the blood." Pancreas highlighted; beta cells release insulin dots.
43–62 s "Insulin acts on liver and muscle cells, helping them take up glucose. Some glucose is stored as glycogen." Liver and muscle take up glucose; glycogen chain forms.
62–76 s "As glucose leaves the blood, blood glucose falls back toward the normal range. The response reduces the original change — that is negative feedback." Graph falls back into range; feedback loop arrow.
76–91 s "Between meals or during exercise, blood glucose can fall. Now alpha cells in the pancreas release glucagon." Graph dips; alpha cells release glucagon.
91–108 s "Glucagon acts mainly on the liver, causing glycogen to break down and glucose to be released into the blood." Liver glycogen breaks into glucose; glucose enters blood.
108–120 s "So your blood glucose is never held at a single number. It's pulled back into range — again, and again, every meal and every hour. That's homeostasis, quietly at work." Time-lapse graph across a whole day: repeated rise-and-return cycles, with the shaded normal range visible throughout.
Exam trap after the video

Do not write that negative feedback prevents blood glucose from changing at all. Blood glucose still changes after meals and exercise. Negative feedback reduces large deviations and returns the level toward the normal range.

3. Interactive learning concept

Title: Move the blood glucose slider

Learner interaction:
Students move a slider from "low blood glucose" to "normal range" to "high blood glucose". The diagram updates to show which hormone is released and how the liver responds.

Slider position Hormone shown Main response Feedback effect
Low glucose Glucagon Liver breaks down glycogen and releases glucose Blood glucose rises
Normal range Low-level adjustment No strong corrective response Level remains within range
High glucose Insulin Liver and muscle take up glucose; glycogen is formed Blood glucose falls

Embedded check:
The learner chooses whether this is positive or negative feedback. The correct answer is negative feedback, because the response reverses the original change.

4. Assessment with marking guidance

Q1. Multiple choice — 1 mark

Which statement best describes negative feedback in blood glucose regulation?

A. The response increases the original change.
B. The response reduces the original change.
C. Blood glucose is kept exactly constant at all times.
D. Insulin and glucagon always increase together.

Answer: B

Feedback: Negative feedback reduces the original change and helps return the variable toward a normal range.

Q2. Short answer — 3 marks

After a meal, blood glucose concentration rises. Explain how insulin helps return blood glucose toward the normal range.

Marking guide:

  • 1 mark: pancreatic beta cells release insulin when blood glucose is high.
  • 1 mark: insulin stimulates target cells such as liver and muscle cells to take up glucose.
  • 1 mark: glucose may be stored as glycogen, reducing blood glucose concentration toward the normal range.

Common 2-mark answer:
The student states that insulin lowers blood glucose but does not explain target tissues or glycogen storage.

Q3. Compare question — 4 marks

Compare the roles of insulin and glucagon in blood glucose homeostasis.

Marking guide:

  • 1 mark: identifies that insulin is released by beta cells when blood glucose is high, whereas glucagon is released by alpha cells when blood glucose is low (or an equivalent explicit contrast in trigger conditions).
  • 1 mark: identifies that insulin lowers blood glucose whereas glucagon raises blood glucose (or an equivalent explicit contrast in overall effect).
  • 1 mark: insulin promotes glucose uptake by target cells and glycogen formation; glucagon stimulates glycogen breakdown in the liver and glucose release into the blood.
  • 1 mark: the response uses comparison language (whereas, in contrast, by contrast, unlike) or otherwise sets the two hormones explicitly against each other, rather than describing each in isolation.

Note on the directive verb:
Compare requires students to set the two hormones against each other, not just describe both in turn. Responses that list "insulin does X. Glucagon does Y." without explicit contrast may lose the final mark even if all the individual facts are correct.

High-quality response example:
Insulin and glucagon have opposing roles in blood glucose homeostasis. Insulin is released by pancreatic beta cells when blood glucose is high, whereas glucagon is released by pancreatic alpha cells when blood glucose is low. Insulin lowers blood glucose by promoting glucose uptake into target cells and glycogen formation, in contrast to glucagon, which raises blood glucose by stimulating glycogen breakdown in the liver and glucose release into the blood. Together they hold blood glucose within a suitable range.

Q4. Data interpretation — 5 marks

A graph shows blood glucose concentration rising sharply after a meal and then returning gradually toward the normal range. Insulin concentration rises shortly after the glucose rise.

Analyse how the graph provides evidence for negative feedback.

Marking guide:

  • 1 mark: identifies the rise in blood glucose after the meal as the stimulus.
  • 1 mark: links the rise in glucose to increased insulin secretion (using the timing in the graph).
  • 1 mark: explains that insulin promotes glucose uptake and/or glycogen storage.
  • 1 mark: links the later fall in blood glucose to the effect of insulin.
  • 1 mark: states that this is negative feedback because the response reduces the original increase in blood glucose.

Answer quality note:
A strong answer refers to the timing in the graph. It should not simply define insulin without using the data.

5. Brief content-design notes

  • Curriculum positioning: VCE Biology Unit 1, Area of Study 2; blood glucose regulation as an example of homeostasis, stimulus-response models and negative feedback.
  • Student-friendly structure: The lesson moves from a familiar experience — eating, exercise and daily glucose changes — into the mechanism of insulin, glucagon, target tissues and feedback.
  • Misconception addressed: Homeostasis is explained as keeping conditions within a suitable range, not holding them exactly constant.
  • Assessment focus: The worked example and questions ask students to use biological reasoning and graph evidence, not just recall hormone names.
  • Human context: The diabetes section is included carefully, focusing on the biology and lived reality without judgement or blame.
