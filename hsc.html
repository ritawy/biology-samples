Curriculum positioning

NSW NESA Biology Stage 6 — Year 12
Module 6: Genetic Change
Focus area: Genetic technologies and artificial manipulation of DNA
Inquiry question: Does artificial manipulation of DNA have the potential to change populations forever?

CRISPR is used here as a worked example, not as a stand-alone topic. It uses CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing as one worked example of a current genetic technology that can induce genetic change. The framing keeps the focus on the syllabus idea: how humans can manipulate DNA, what this allows us to do, and why the consequences need careful evaluation.

Learning objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to:

  1. explain how CRISPR-Cas9 can induce a targeted genetic change;
  2. distinguish the roles of guide RNA, Cas9 and DNA repair;
  3. compare two possible repair outcomes after a CRISPR-induced DNA break;
  4. connect somatic and germline editing to inheritance and population-level change; and
  5. evaluate one advantage and one risk of using a genetic technology in a real context.

Prior knowledge

Students should already understand that:

  • DNA is a sequence of bases carrying genetic information.
  • Complementary base pairing allows nucleic acid sequences to match.
  • A mutation is a change in a DNA sequence.
  • Changes in DNA can alter proteins and phenotype.
  • Inherited changes can affect future generations.

1. Text lesson — Find, cut, repair

Why this matters

A small change in DNA can matter a lot. It may change the structure of a protein, affect a crop's resistance to disease, or sit behind an inherited condition in a family. Genetic technologies matter because they give humans a way to make DNA changes deliberately rather than waiting for random mutation or many generations of selective breeding.

That power is exciting. It is also why the science needs to be explained carefully.

The big idea

CRISPR-Cas9 is a gene-editing system that can be designed to cut DNA at a chosen target sequence.

It has two main parts:

  • Guide RNA: a short RNA sequence that helps locate the target DNA sequence by complementary base pairing.
  • Cas9: an enzyme that cuts both strands of DNA near the target.

A useful summary is:

Guide RNA finds. Cas9 cuts. The cell repairs.

The last step is important. CRISPR does not "decide" the final edit by itself. Cas9 makes a targeted break, but the final genetic change depends on how the cell repairs that break.

Step 1 — Find the target sequence

Scientists design a guide RNA with a base sequence complementary to the target DNA. When the guide RNA matches the target region, it helps bring Cas9 to that location.

The guide RNA is a little like an address label. The analogy helps, but it has a limit: an address label does not repair a house. In the same way, guide RNA helps locate the DNA target, but it does not fix the gene.

Quick check 1

If scientists change the guide RNA sequence, what is most likely to change?

A. The target DNA sequence recognised by the system
B. The amino acid sequence of Cas9
C. The number of chromosomes in the cell
D. The type of sugar in DNA

Answer: A. The guide RNA sequence affects which DNA sequence is recognised.

Step 2 — Cut the DNA

Once the guide RNA binds near the target, Cas9 creates a double-strand break in the DNA.

This cut is the induced genetic change that starts the editing process. However, the cut is not the final outcome. Broken DNA is risky for a cell, so the cell activates repair pathways.

Step 3 — Repair creates the final change

Cells repair double-strand breaks in more than one way. Scientists use this repair response to create different outcomes.

Outcome A — Gene disruption

If the repair is quick but imperfect, small bases may be inserted or deleted. These small changes can disrupt the reading frame or alter the gene sequence so the gene no longer produces a functional protein.

This is useful when researchers want to switch off a gene and study what changes.

Outcome B — Gene correction or insertion

If scientists provide a repair template, the cell may copy that template into the DNA during repair. This can replace a disease-causing sequence or insert a desired version of a gene.

This is useful when the aim is not simply to switch a gene off, but to change it to a more useful or functional version.

Quick check 2

Why is the repair step so important in CRISPR-Cas9 editing?

Model answer: The repair step determines the final genetic change. Repair may disrupt the gene through small insertions or deletions, or it may copy a supplied template to produce a corrected or new sequence.

Common misconception

Misconception: CRISPR automatically fixes a faulty gene once Cas9 cuts the DNA.

Correction: Cas9 creates a targeted double-strand break. The final result depends on the cell's repair pathway, whether a repair template is supplied, and whether the guide RNA has matched the intended target accurately. CRISPR is powerful, but it is not perfect or risk-free.

Worked example (equivalent to a ~3-mark short-answer response)

A disease-causing allele contains a single-base mutation that changes the shape of a protein. Scientists design a guide RNA that matches the DNA sequence near the mutation. They also provide a repair template containing the normal version of the sequence.

Question: Explain how CRISPR-Cas9 could be used to produce the corrected allele.

Worked response:
The guide RNA base-pairs with the target DNA sequence near the mutation and directs Cas9 to that region. Cas9 creates a double-strand break in the DNA. The cell then attempts to repair the break. If the supplied repair template is used during repair, the normal base sequence can be copied into the genome. This can replace the disease-causing sequence and produce a corrected allele.

Why this is a strong answer:
It separates the roles of guide RNA, Cas9 and the repair template. It also explains the sequence of events rather than simply saying "CRISPR fixes the gene".

Uses and advantages

CRISPR is useful because it can be designed to target a specific sequence more easily than many older genetic technologies.

Medicine

CRISPR can help scientists investigate disease genes and is being developed for some genetic conditions. A careful answer should avoid saying it is already a simple cure for all genetic disease. A stronger answer explains that it may allow researchers to disrupt, correct or replace a sequence in specific contexts.

Agriculture

CRISPR can be used to produce crops with useful traits, such as disease resistance or drought tolerance. In some cases, this may involve changing an organism's own DNA rather than inserting a gene from a different species.

Research

Researchers can switch off a gene to investigate its function. If the cell's behaviour changes after the gene is disrupted, that provides evidence about what the gene normally does.

Ethics and population-level change

The Module 6 inquiry question asks whether artificial manipulation of DNA can change populations forever. CRISPR links directly to this question, especially when students compare somatic and germline editing.

  • Somatic cell editing affects body cells. The change may help the treated individual but is not passed to offspring.
  • Germline editing affects reproductive cells or embryos. The change may be inherited by future generations.

That difference is why germline editing raises much larger ethical concerns. It is not only a technical question. It involves consent from future generations, unequal access to expensive treatments, possible off-target effects, and the question of which traits society should or should not try to change.

A thoughtful exam response should not simply say "CRISPR is good" or "CRISPR is bad". It should weigh the benefit, the risk, the type of cell being edited, and the purpose of the technology.

2. Video script — CRISPR in about 105 seconds

Time Narration On-screen / visual direction
0–10 s "A tiny change in DNA can change the way a protein works. Genetic technologies matter because they let scientists make some DNA changes deliberately." Cell → nucleus → chromosome → DNA sequence. One base is highlighted.
10–24 s "CRISPR-Cas9 is one example. It has two key parts: a guide RNA that finds the target sequence, and Cas9, an enzyme that cuts DNA." Guide RNA and Cas9 appear as labelled parts.
24–40 s "The guide RNA is designed to match a chosen DNA sequence by complementary base pairing. It helps bring Cas9 to the right region of the genome." Base-pairing animation; target region lights up.
40–54 s "Cas9 then creates a double-strand break. That cut starts the editing process — but it is not the final edit." DNA is cut; label: double-strand break.
54–73 s "Now the cell repairs the break. If repair is imperfect, small bases may be added or lost, which can disrupt the gene." Repair pathway 1: insertion/deletion → gene disrupted.
73–91 s "If a repair template is supplied, the cell may copy that template, producing a corrected or new sequence." Repair pathway 2: template copied into DNA.
91–108 s "So the pattern is: guide RNA finds, Cas9 cuts, the cell repairs. The science is powerful, but the consequences depend on the target, the repair outcome and whether the change can be inherited." FIND → CUT → REPAIR. End card: Genetic Technologies.
Exam trap after the video

Do not write that the guide RNA repairs DNA. The guide RNA helps locate the target. Cas9 cuts the DNA. The cell's repair process produces the final genetic change.

3. Assessment with marking guidance

Q1. Multiple choice — 1 mark

What is the role of guide RNA in CRISPR-Cas9 editing?

A. It cuts both DNA strands.
B. It helps direct Cas9 to a target DNA sequence.
C. It repairs the double-strand break.
D. It converts DNA into protein.

Answer: B

Feedback: Cas9 cuts the DNA. The cell repairs the break. Guide RNA helps target the system to a particular DNA sequence.

Q2. Short answer — 3 marks

Outline how CRISPR-Cas9 can induce a genetic change at a specific location in the genome.

Marking guide:

  • 1 mark: guide RNA matches or binds to a complementary target DNA sequence.
  • 1 mark: Cas9 creates a double-strand break at or near the target site.
  • 1 mark: DNA repair produces the final genetic change, such as gene disruption or copying a supplied repair template.

Common 2-mark answer:
The student describes guide RNA and Cas9 but does not explain that DNA repair creates the final edited sequence.

Q3. Explain question — 4 marks

A researcher wants to switch off a particular gene to investigate its function. Explain how CRISPR-Cas9 could be used in this investigation.

Marking guide:

  • 1 mark: a guide RNA is designed with a sequence complementary to the target gene, directing Cas9 to that site.
  • 1 mark: Cas9 creates a double-strand break at or near the target sequence.
  • 1 mark: imperfect repair may introduce small insertions or deletions that disrupt the reading frame or coding sequence, so the gene no longer produces a functional protein.
  • 1 mark: links the disrupted gene to the research aim — for example, observing how the cell's structure, behaviour or phenotype changes once the gene is switched off provides evidence about its normal function.

Common 3-mark answer:
The student explains the guide RNA, Cas9 and repair steps accurately, but does not connect the gene disruption back to the research aim of investigating gene function.

Q4. Extended response — 5 marks

A research team uses CRISPR-Cas9 to develop a crop variety that is resistant to a fungal disease. Evaluate one advantage and one risk of using this genetic technology compared with traditional selective breeding.

Marking guide:

  • 1 mark: identifies a relevant advantage, such as speed, precision or ability to target a known gene.
  • 1 mark: explains the advantage in comparison with selective breeding.
  • 1 mark: identifies a relevant risk, such as off-target edits, ecological effects, gene flow to related plants, or regulatory uncertainty.
  • 1 mark: explains why the risk matters in this agricultural context specifically (e.g. open field release, contact with wild relatives).
  • 1 mark: makes an evaluative judgement that weighs the advantage against the risk, with conditions for when the technology would or would not be justified.

High-quality response example:
CRISPR can be advantageous because it can target a known fungal-resistance gene directly, producing the desired trait in fewer generations than selective breeding, and without necessarily introducing unwanted traits from parent plants. However, the risk of off-target edits or gene flow into wild relatives matters more in this agricultural context than in a contained laboratory setting: edited resistance genes that spread into wild populations could alter natural fungal–plant relationships, and once released, such changes are difficult to reverse. On balance, CRISPR is likely to be justified for fungal-resistance crops if off-target effects have been minimised and verified, the edited trait is unlikely to confer a fitness advantage to wild relatives, and ongoing environmental monitoring is in place. Speed and precision do not, by themselves, make the technology safe once it leaves the laboratory.

4. Notes on content design

  • Syllabus-safe framing: The lesson is about genetic technologies and induced genetic change; CRISPR is used as a worked example, not treated as the whole module.
  • Warm but precise tone: The lesson starts from why DNA change matters to people, crops and health, while avoiding hype or overpromising.
  • Mechanism before judgement: Students first learn find-cut-repair, then use that mechanism to evaluate benefits and risks.
  • Misconceptions made visible: The lesson directly corrects the common idea that CRISPR automatically "fixes" genes.
  • Directive-verb-aware assessment: Items use outline, explain and evaluate with marking guides that match the depth each verb requires, and each question signals what a partial-credit response typically looks like.
